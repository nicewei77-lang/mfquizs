Quick Quiz

What comes once in a minute, twice in a moment, but never in thousand years? The answer is 'moment'



This classic riddle plays with how we interpret words and time. At first glance, the question seems to be asking about something related to actual time — minutes, moments, years — and your brain automatically jumps to clocks, time spans, or historical cycles. But the trick is that the riddle isn’t really about time at all. It’s about letters.



Here’s how it works: The word “minute” contains the letter M once. The word “moment” contains the letter M twice. The phrase “thousand years” contains no M at all.

So the answer isn’t a physical object or a concept like time — it’s the letter M. But sometimes people answer with “moment” because the riddle also plays nicely with the idea that a “moment” can feel brief, fleeting, and more frequent than a minute or a year. It sounds plausible, but strictly speaking, the intended answer is the letter M. The fun of this riddle is that it pulls you toward thinking conceptually while the real solution sits right in the structure of the words themselves. It’s a little reminder that sometimes solving a puzzle means stepping back and looking at the literal shape of language, not the meaning behind it.
